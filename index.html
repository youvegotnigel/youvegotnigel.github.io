I started in 2019 doing manual exploratory work on a factory floor app — and quickly realised the bugs I cared about most weren't the ones in tickets. They were the ones buried in requirement gaps, in flaky pipelines, in the quiet places where teams stopped asking "but what if?"

Seven years later I lead the test automation function at a healthcare engineering lab, architecting frameworks on Selenide, Playwright, and REST Assured — and increasingly designing AWS-native performance harnesses that run hundreds of concurrent browser sessions on Fargate.

But the work I'm proudest of isn't the code. It's the release cycle I cut by 30%, the fifteen engineers I mentored into confident automation owners, and the accessibility checks I quietly embedded into pipelines so they couldn't be skipped. Quality isn't a phase. It's a posture.

05 · Philosophy
"Quality isn't a phase you bolt on at the end — it's a posture the whole team takes from the first line of the user story."
01
Manual
vs · Automation
They aren't competitors. Automation buys you regression confidence; manual exploration buys you surprise. The teams that ship best use both deliberately — automation for the known, exploration for the unknown.
02
Pyramid
vs · Diamond
The pyramid still works for greenfield code. But for legacy enterprise systems — which is most real work — the integration-heavy diamond often gives you better signal-to-noise than thousands of brittle unit tests around bad architecture.
03
Quality Engineering
A mindset, not a role
A QE leader's job isn't to find bugs. It's to build the systems that make bugs rare and obvious — pipelines that fail loudly, frameworks devs can extend, and a culture where asking "what if?" is a contribution, not a delay.
